A Skincare Perspective To Working with Your Menstrual Cycle

Navigating skincare while dealing with hormonal fluctuations can be challenging. Inspired by a question about whether those with oily skin follow different routines around their periods, I've delved into how our menstrual cycles can impact skin health. My personal experience with acne, sensitive skin, and PMDD (Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder) led me on a journey to understand how to work with, rather than against, my cycle.

For years, my skin would flare up in the two weeks before my period, making it seem like all my progress was lost each month. Despite dermatologists attributing it to hormonal acne, I was determined to find solutions. I eventually learned to align my skincare routine with my cycle, a concept often referred to as "cycle syncing" or "cycle mapping."

Tracking Your Cycle

First and foremost, I strongly recommend tracking your cycle. Using an app like Clue, which I've relied on for years, has been invaluable. Tracking allowed me to detect a serious health issue early on, leading to timely treatment and a clean bill of health. If there's one takeaway from this post, it’s to track your period.

Understanding the Phases

There are four distinct phases in the menstrual cycle, each influencing skin health differently:

1. Week 1: Menstruation – Soothe and Hydrate

During menstruation, with low estrogen and progesterone, your skin may be at its most sensitive. Focus on soothing and repairing. Use products you know work for you and avoid introducing new ones. Post-period, hydrate well as prostaglandins can cause dehydration. It’s a time for extra nourishment and support.

2. Week 2: Follicular Phase – Stimulate and Experiment

This is often when you feel at your best. Your skin is resilient and glowing. It’s the ideal time to try new products and introduce actives like vitamin C. Pain tolerance is also higher, making it a good week for waxing, threading, and even more intensive procedures like microneedling or peels.

3. Day 14/Mid-Cycle: Ovulation – Clear Pores and Prevent Acne

During ovulation, with the highest estrogen levels and a testosterone bump, your skin is at its peak glow. However, a spike in testosterone might cause a few breakouts. Focus on exfoliation and pore-clearing treatments. This is the time to use products like The Ordinary’s Peeling Solution or thorough oil cleansing to prevent acne.

4. Week 3: Luteal Phase – Oil Control

As estrogen levels drop and progesterone rises, you might experience increased oiliness. This phase is all about controlling oil and using gentle chemical exfoliation. Opt for clay masks and BHAs, and adjust your cleansing routine to handle the excess oil. Hydrate well but avoid heavy moisturizers.

5. Week 4: Pre-Menstrual – Cool and Soothe Inflammation

With low estrogen and high progesterone, your skin may be sensitive and oily. Avoid new products and stick to soothing ones that you know work. Cooling face packs and aloe gel masks can help alleviate sensitivity and prevent breakouts. Avoid extreme temperatures and focus on gentle care.

In Summary

Understanding how your skin responds throughout your menstrual cycle can make a significant difference in managing acne and maintaining skin health. While these guidelines offer a general approach, each person’s cycle and skin are unique. Observing and adapting based on your own experiences will help you find what works best for you.

For those struggling with severe acne, hormonal breakouts, or conditions like PCOS, consulting a dermatologist is crucial. Tracking your cycle and adjusting your skincare routine accordingly can provide valuable insights into managing your skin health.

Embracing and working with your natural cycle rather than against it can lead to a more harmonious and effective skincare routine. I hope these insights help you achieve a healthier, more balanced approach to skincare.
