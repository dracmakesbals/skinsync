Sunscreen 101: Everything You Need to Know for Effective UV Protection

Disclaimer: I’m not a dermatologist or an aesthetician. I’m just a skincare enthusiast who’s fairly new to this community. If I’m wrong about any of this, please feel free to correct me with research-backed information.

Why Wear Sunscreen?

We’ve all heard it before: “Wear sunscreen!” But why is it so crucial? Sunscreen helps protect our skin by reducing the amount of UV radiation that reaches it. Sunscreen molecules absorb UV energy and convert it into less harmful forms. This shields your skin from sun damage, which can lead to premature aging and skin cancer.

Understanding UV Rays: UVA vs. UVB

UV rays come in two types:

  • UVB Rays: Shorter wavelengths that cause sunburn and are linked to skin cancer. They don’t penetrate the skin as deeply.
  • UVA Rays: Longer wavelengths responsible for premature aging, deeper skin damage, and hyperpigmentation.

What Do SPF and PA Mean?

SPF (Sun Protection Factor): Indicates UVB protection.

PA (Protection Grade of UVA): Indicates UVA protection.

Physical vs. Chemical Sunscreens

Physical (Inorganic) Sunscreens: Contain minerals like Zinc Oxide and Titanium Dioxide. They sit on the skin’s surface and reflect UV rays.

Chemical (Organic) Sunscreens: Contain ingredients like Oxybenzone, Avobenzone, and Octocrylene. They absorb UV rays and convert them into heat, which is then released from the skin.

Choosing the Right Sunscreen

The best sunscreen is the one you’ll actually use. Chemical sunscreens often feel lighter and are less likely to leave a white cast, making them popular. However, physical sunscreens are often better for sensitive skin and can be less irritating.

How Much Sunscreen to Apply

You should use at least 1/4 teaspoon or two finger lengths of sunscreen for your face. Applying more is always safer to ensure full coverage.

Sunscreen and Makeup

Most makeup products with SPF don’t provide enough coverage for complete sun protection. It’s best to apply a dedicated sunscreen first.

Sunscreen in Moisturizers

Moisturizers with SPF are convenient, but ensure you apply enough to get the full benefit. Personal experience suggests that using these can sometimes result in less application due to the greasy feeling.

Reapplication and Waiting Time

Sunscreen needs to be reapplied every two hours, especially if you’re sweating or exposed to the sun for long periods. Although sunscreens start working immediately, wait for them to dry completely before applying makeup.

Addressing Common Concerns

  • Chemical Sunscreens and Hormone Disruption: The concentration required to cause disruptions is higher than what you’d absorb from sunscreen.
  • Nanoparticles in Physical Filters: Studies show that they don’t penetrate deeply into the skin, though they could theoretically be absorbed if you have broken skin.
  • Blue Light Protection: Sunscreens don’t protect against blue light, but screens emit insufficient blue light to cause skin damage.

Indoor Sunscreen Use

While UVB rays are mostly blocked by glass, about 1/3 to 1/4 of UVA rays still pass through. If you spend a lot of time near windows, indoor sunscreen might still be beneficial.

The Benefits and Cons of Sunscreen

Benefits of UV Exposure: Vitamin D production from UVB exposure is a benefit of sunlight. However, if you’re using products like AHAs or retinoids, or have photosensitive skin, it’s wise to stick to sunscreen.

The Cons of Sunscreen: While essential, sunscreen tubes often seem too small and don’t last long. Larger packaging could reduce waste and be more cost-effective. Many skincare products, including sunscreens, are still sold in sizes that lead to frequent repurchasing and increased costs.
